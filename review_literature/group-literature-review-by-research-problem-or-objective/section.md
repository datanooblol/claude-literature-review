## 3. Research Problems in Retrieval-Augmented Generation: A Problem-Oriented Survey

Retrieval-Augmented Generation has matured into a distinct research area with a recognizable set of failure modes. Rather than organizing prior work by methodology or pipeline stage, this section groups the reviewed literature by the specific problem each paper targets. This framing reveals which failure modes have attracted concentrated research effort, which remain underexplored, and where competing solutions can be directly compared. Five problem clusters emerge: query-side failures before retrieval, retrieval design choices, post-retrieval context quality, context length as a fundamental barrier, and knowledge integration beyond in-context injection.

---

### 3.1 Query-Side Failures and Pre-Retrieval Augmentation

A prerequisite for effective retrieval is a query that the retriever can match against. In general-domain settings this is rarely a problem, but in industrial knowledge bases — where user queries are dense with domain jargon, abbreviations, and implicit context — the query itself is the primary failure point. An et al. (2024) demonstrate that standard corrective and self-reflective RAG methods (Corrective RAG, Self-RAG) cannot compensate when the initial retrieval is corrupted at the query stage, because post-retrieval corrections cannot recover documents that were never returned.

Golden-Retriever addresses this through a four-step reflection pipeline executed before any retrieval call: an LLM identifies jargon and abbreviations in the query, a code-synthesized SQL lookup retrieves definitions from a maintained jargon dictionary, a CoT-based classifier assigns the query a context category (e.g., "Design Question", "Process Question"), and the augmented query replaces the original for retrieval. Evaluated on 58 multiple-choice questions drawn from Western Digital's new-hire training materials, this pre-retrieval augmentation yields a 57.3% average improvement over a vanilla LLM baseline and a 35.0% improvement over standard RAG, across three LLMs, with no model training. The approach is notable for what it does not require: no fine-tuning, no architectural changes, and no modification to the retriever or generator.

This cluster is sparsely populated in the reviewed literature — Golden-Retriever is the only paper whose primary contribution lies at the query stage. This likely reflects a selection bias toward general-domain benchmarks in the field, where query ambiguity is less pronounced, rather than an absence of the underlying problem.

### 3.2 Retrieval Design: Context Size, Corpus, and Method

A second cluster of papers treats the retrieval step itself as the primary variable and asks how design choices — how much to retrieve, from where, and with what method — jointly affect downstream QA quality. Unlike Problem 1, where the query is broken, these papers assume the query is reasonable and ask whether current retrieval practice is well-calibrated.

Vladika and Matthes (2025) conduct the most systematic study in this cluster, varying the number of retrieved snippets (k = 0 to 30), the retrieval corpus size (closed 8k-document set vs. open 10-million-document corpus), and the retrieval algorithm (BM25 vs. semantic search) across eight LLMs on two long-form QA benchmarks. Their findings challenge several common assumptions. Performance improves steadily up to approximately 15 snippets before saturating or declining — confirming a context ceiling effect. Domain fit of the LLM matters more than model size: Mixtral-8x7B and Mistral-7B outperform GPT-4o on biomedical QA despite being smaller models, while the ranking reverses for encyclopedic tasks. Most strikingly, for some model-dataset combinations, providing poorly retrieved snippets actively hurts performance relative to relying on the model's parametric knowledge alone — a finding that complicates the assumption that more retrieval is always better.

Merola and Singh (2025) examine retrieval design at the indexing level, comparing chunking strategies that determine the unit of retrieval. Traditional early chunking (segment then embed) fragments semantic context: a chunk reading "The company's revenue grew by 3%" carries no information about which company. Late chunking (embed the full document first, then segment) attempts to preserve global context, as does contextual retrieval (prepend an LLM-generated context summary to each chunk before embedding). Under controlled conditions on NFCorpus and MsMarco, contextual retrieval with rank fusion consistently outperforms late chunking on retrieval metrics. However, the margin is modest, and the cost is substantial: LLM inference per chunk and up to 20GB of GPU memory for long documents. The practical conclusion is that chunking strategy is a resource-budget decision as much as a quality decision.

Li et al. (2025) complement these findings through a 74-run ablation across nine RAG design dimensions including chunk size, knowledge base size, retrieval stride, and query expansion. Their most striking result is the prevalence of null findings: chunk size, KB size, retrieval stride, and query expansion all have minimal impact on downstream performance. What does matter is the in-context learning strategy at generation time — their novel Contrastive ICL configuration, which provides both correct and incorrect examples in context, improves ROUGE-L from 8.91 to 23.87 and FActScore from 63.73 to 74.44 on MMLU. The implication is that many standard levers for tuning RAG systems at the retrieval stage are essentially irrelevant compared to how the generator uses the retrieved content.

### 3.3 Post-Retrieval Context Quality and Compression

The largest problem cluster in this corpus — three papers — targets the gap between what the retriever returns and what the generator can effectively use. Retrieved chunks typically contain noise, redundancy, and evidence scattered across multiple documents; the generator must resolve these imperfections without any guarantee that the retrieval and generation components have been jointly optimized. Three papers address this from different angles: formal diagnosis, learned compression, and architectural compression.

Joren et al. (2025) establish the diagnostic foundation by introducing a formal notion of sufficient context — whether the retrieved content contains enough information to answer the query, independent of whether the LLM can actually use it. Prior work conflated topically related with answer-sufficient content, making it impossible to separate retrieval failure from generation failure. Using a Gemini 1.5 Pro autorater that achieves 93% accuracy on this classification task (without requiring ground truth answers), they stratify responses across FreshQA, Musique, and HotpotQA into correct, abstain, and hallucinate categories, conditioned on context sufficiency. The key findings are sobering: even when context is sufficient, frontier models (Gemini 1.5 Pro, GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet) still hallucinate at 14–17%; with insufficient context, models hallucinate far more than they abstain, suggesting they are systematically overconfident when given any retrieved content. Their selective generation method — gating responses on a combined sufficiency signal and model self-confidence score — improves accuracy by 2–10% across coverage levels with no fine-tuning required.

Li and Ramakrishnan (2025) take a learning-based approach, introducing Oreo as a plug-in T5-small module inserted between retriever and generator. Trained through three progressive stages — supervised fine-tuning on LLM-generated gold contexts, contrastive multi-task learning over beam-search-ranked candidates, and PPO-based alignment to the downstream generator's preferences — Oreo reconstructs retrieved chunks into a concise, query-specific context before generation. The RL alignment stage addresses a limitation that the prior stages cannot: the retriever, reconstructor, and generator are independently optimized, and the distributional mismatch cannot be resolved by gradient backpropagation when the generator is a black box. Across five QA datasets, Oreo achieves up to 37.5% EM improvement on NaturalQuestions and 19.8% F1 improvement on 2WikiMultihopQA while compressing context by 84–94% and reducing generation latency by 23–43%. Its robustness to chunk order shuffling suggests it operates as an implicit reranker — resolving the "lost-in-the-middle" problem as a secondary benefit.

Lin et al. (2025) approach post-retrieval quality from the architectural side, motivated by a structural observation: retrieved passages are semantically diverse due to deduplication, producing block-diagonal attention patterns in which cross-passage attention is near-zero. This means the quadratic attention computation over retrieved content is largely redundant. REFRAG replaces retrieved passage tokens with pre-computable encoder embeddings (RoBERTa-base projected through a 2-layer MLP) before they reach the decoder, reducing input length by a factor of k. Three-stage training — reconstruction pre-training with curriculum learning, continual pre-training on next-paragraph prediction, and RL-based selective compression — enables adaptive selection of which chunks to compress at inference time. At compression rate k=32, REFRAG achieves 30.85× TTFT acceleration over standard LLaMA and 3.75× over the previous SOTA (CEPE), with matching perplexity. The pre-computability of chunk embeddings is a key practical property: the encoding cost is paid once per document at index time, not once per query at inference time.

### 3.4 Context Length as a Fundamental Barrier

The fourth cluster is distinguished from Problem 3 by its framing: rather than asking how to improve the quality of what is fed to the generator, it asks whether the generator can reliably use long inputs at all — and finds that it cannot, for reasons that are more fundamental than retrieval design or context quality.

Du et al. (2025) provide the most direct evidence. They construct a controlled synthetic benchmark in which evidence and question are separated by distractor tokens, then systematically vary distractor type (natural text, whitespace, masked attention) and length across five models and four tasks. Retrieval success is verified by exact match before analysis. Across all conditions, accuracy degrades monotonically as context grows — falling between 13.9% and 85% relative to the short-context baseline. The masking condition is particularly telling: even when all distractors are masked and the model can only attend to evidence and question, accuracy still drops consistently (at least 7.9% for both open-source models at 30k masked tokens, with Llama-HumanEval dropping 50%). The authors attribute this to a positional distribution mismatch: LLMs trained on short-context data develop positional encodings that degrade when evidence and question are far apart, regardless of whether intervening tokens are informative. This finding reframes the RAG degradation problem: it is not only about retrieval quality or context noise, but about a fundamental limitation in how LLMs process positional distance.

Roberts et al. (2025) extend this diagnosis to near-million-scale contexts with a synthetic UUID key-value benchmark that progressively increases task difficulty from single needle retrieval to multi-step threading across 17 LLMs at up to 630k tokens. Their effective context length metric — expressed in characters to enable fair cross-model comparison — reveals that most models' practical limits lie far below their advertised context windows. For threading tasks at five steps, all models except GPT-4o and LLaMA 3.1 405B achieve zero effective context length. A notable positive finding is that models are "thread-safe" in the synthetic setting: tracking multiple simultaneous chains does not degrade per-chain accuracy. However, this advantage disappears in natural language haystacks, where semantic noise reintroduces the failures that structured UUID tasks mask.

Bansal et al. (2025) provide a theoretical account of the same phenomenon, formalizing it as score dilution: when many distractor tokens are near-tied with the target in logit space, the softmax attention mass on the target vanishes as context length T grows, with the required logit gap scaling as Ω(log T). They further prove that autoregressively generated thinking tokens cannot repair this deficit — each thinking token carries at most its own attention mass on the target. Their proposed remedy, query-only test-time training (qTTT), reallocates inference-time compute from generating more thinking tokens to a small number of gradient steps that update only the query projection matrices W_Q while keeping the KV cache frozen. This provably increases the target–distractor logit margin. On LongBench-v2 with Qwen3-4B, qTTT achieves 39.6% accuracy against 33.5% for FLOP-matched thinking tokens and 27.0% for standard in-context, with the largest gains on retrieval-driven tasks (Multi-Document QA: 30.0 → 46.0). The practical implication is a reallocation of inference budget: for long-context tasks, adapting query projections is a better use of compute than producing longer reasoning chains.

### 3.5 Knowledge Integration Beyond In-Context Injection

The final cluster contains a single paper that challenges the foundational assumption shared by all other works in this corpus: that external knowledge should be injected through the input context. Su et al. (2025) argue that this paradigm has two structural limitations — long contexts increase computational overhead and degrade performance on complex reasoning tasks, and in-context injection only affects attention-layer KV computations rather than the FFN parameters where the model's knowledge is actually stored. The implication is that LLMs can never use externally provided context as effectively as their internalized parametric knowledge.

Parametric RAG addresses this by training a LoRA adapter for each document offline, encoding document content directly into FFN weight perturbations, then merging the adapters of retrieved documents into the LLM's weights before generation — leaving the input context empty. On factoid and multi-hop QA benchmarks, P-RAG outperforms all in-context RAG baselines with 29–36% lower inference latency for LLaMA-3-8B. Combining P-RAG with standard in-context RAG achieves the highest overall performance, suggesting the two paradigms are complementary rather than competitive. The primary limitations are storage cost (approximately 4.72 MB per document in LoRA matrices) and the model-specificity of parametric representations, making the approach most suitable for fixed, slowly-changing corpora with high query volumes.

This problem cluster is the most isolated in the reviewed literature — no other paper directly challenges or extends the parametric injection framing. It represents either a nascent research direction yet to attract follow-on work, or a fundamental architectural constraint that the field has implicitly decided to work around rather than through.

---

### 3.6 Cross-Cutting Observations

Three patterns emerge across all five problem clusters. First, the boundary between retrieval failure and generation failure is harder to draw than commonly assumed: Joren et al. (2025) show that sufficient context still produces hallucination, while Du et al. (2025) show that perfect retrieval still produces performance degradation. Framing RAG failures as either "retrieval" or "generation" errors is likely too coarse. Second, the Problem 2 null results (chunk size, query expansion, retrieval stride all minimally impactful) suggest that the field may have been optimizing the wrong stage: retrieval design appears to matter less than what happens after retrieval. Third, Problems 3 and 4 overlap in a way that no reviewed paper addresses explicitly — if context length alone degrades LLM reasoning (Problem 4), then compressing context (Problem 3) may treat a symptom rather than the root cause, and the right solution may require interventions at the model architecture level (as qTTT and Parametric RAG attempt).
