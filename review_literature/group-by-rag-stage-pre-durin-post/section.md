## 3. A Pipeline-Stage Survey of Retrieval-Augmented Generation Research

A RAG system can be decomposed into three sequential stages: preparation before retrieval (document indexing and query formulation), the retrieval step itself, and post-retrieval processing through to generation. This section surveys the reviewed literature through that lens. The pipeline-stage framing is the most actionable for system designers — it maps research contributions directly to intervention points in a deployable system. A notable structural feature of this corpus is the uneven distribution of research effort across stages: the post-retrieval stage attracts by far the most papers, reflecting the field's implicit assumption that retrieval is a solved or solvable problem and that the bottleneck lies in what the LLM does with retrieved content.

---

### 3.1 Stage 1: Pre-Retrieval — Document Indexing and Query Preparation

Before any retrieval occurs, two preparatory decisions shape everything downstream: how documents are indexed (chunked, embedded, and stored) and how the user's query is formulated or transformed before being sent to the retriever. Two papers focus primarily on this stage; a third (Parametric RAG) also belongs here in part.

**Query preparation.** An, Ding, et al. (2024) identify query formulation as the primary failure point in industrial RAG deployments. When user queries contain domain-specific jargon, abbreviations, and implicit context that the retriever cannot match, no amount of post-retrieval correction can compensate — the relevant documents were never returned. Golden-Retriever addresses this through a reflection pipeline executed before retrieval: an LLM extracts jargon terms, a SQL lookup against a maintained jargon dictionary retrieves definitions, a CoT classifier assigns the query a context category, and an augmented query is constructed before the retrieval call is made. Evaluated on a 58-question benchmark from Western Digital's industrial knowledge base, this pre-retrieval augmentation yields a 57.3% improvement over vanilla LLM and 35.0% over standard RAG across three LLMs — with no model training. The result establishes query-side intervention as a high-leverage, low-cost action when domain jargon is present.

**Document indexing.** Merola and Singh (2025) study how chunking strategy — the way documents are segmented before embedding — affects both retrieval quality and downstream generation. Fixed-size chunking (segment then embed) fragments semantic context: isolated chunks lose the information needed to interpret them. Two alternatives aim to preserve global context: late chunking embeds the full document first and then segments the resulting token embeddings, while contextual retrieval prepends an LLM-generated summary to each chunk before embedding. Under controlled experiments on NFCorpus and MsMarco, contextual retrieval with rank fusion outperforms late chunking on NDCG@5 and MAP@5, but the improvement comes at a steep cost — LLM inference per chunk and up to 20GB of GPU memory for long documents. Late chunking is efficient but inconsistent across embedding models; BGE-M3 shows early (traditional) chunking substantially outperforming it. The practical implication is that chunking strategy is governed as much by available infrastructure as by quality objectives.

**Offline knowledge parameterization.** Su et al. (2025) introduce a more radical pre-retrieval intervention: training a LoRA adapter per document offline, encoding the document's knowledge directly into model weight perturbations rather than into an indexed embedding. This offline parameterization step — which involves document augmentation (rewriting + QA pair generation) followed by LoRA training — belongs to Stage 1 because it determines the form in which knowledge is stored before any query arrives. Its consequences for the retrieval and generation stages are discussed in Section 3.3.

### 3.2 Stage 2: Retrieval — Fetch Design and Corpus Configuration

The retrieval step — selecting which documents or passages to surface for a given query — involves several configurable decisions: retrieval algorithm (sparse vs. dense), number of retrieved snippets (k), and corpus scope (closed vs. open domain). Two papers study these decisions systematically; a third examines them as part of a broader ablation.

Vladika and Matthes (2025) conduct the most controlled study of retrieval design variables in this corpus, systematically varying k from 0 to 30 snippets, comparing BM25 against semantic search, and contrasting retrieval from a closed 8,000-document corpus against an open 10-million-document corpus across eight LLMs on BioASQ and QuoteSum. Several findings are non-obvious. Performance improves steadily up to approximately 15 snippets but saturates or declines beyond that — establishing a practical ceiling for how much retrieval is useful. BM25 outperforms semantic search for biomedical QA, where keyword precision matters more than semantic breadth. And for some model-dataset combinations, poorly retrieved snippets actively hurt performance relative to using the model's parametric knowledge alone, with GPT-4o and Mixtral scoring higher at k=0 (no retrieval) than at low k with imperfect retrieval — a finding that challenges the assumption that any retrieval is better than none.

Li et al. (2025) examine retrieval design as one dimension in a 74-run ablation across nine RAG configuration choices. Their retrieval-related findings are largely null: query expansion, retrieval stride (how frequently context is refreshed during generation), chunk size, and knowledge base size all have minimal impact on downstream performance. The most effective intervention in their study — Contrastive ICL, which provides correct and incorrect examples in the generator's context — is a generation-stage choice, not a retrieval-stage one. The implication is that standard retrieval tuning levers have limited headroom and that practitioner effort may be better directed elsewhere.

Merola and Singh (2025) also contribute to this stage through their evaluation of rank fusion: combining dense embedding similarity with BM25 sparse scores (at a 4:1 weight ratio) consistently outperforms either retrieval signal alone, and the inclusion of a cross-encoder reranker is critical to realizing this gain. Without reranking, rank fusion does not reliably improve results.

Taken together, Stage 2 research presents a cautionary picture: retrieval design can be harmful (bad snippets hurt), is subject to diminishing returns (more snippets plateau quickly), and its standard tuning levers (query expansion, chunk size) are mostly inert.

### 3.3 Stage 3: Post-Retrieval — Context Processing and Generation

Seven of the eleven reviewed papers make their primary contribution at Stage 3 — the period after retrieval when the LLM must process the retrieved content and produce a response. This concentration reflects a shared assumption: that retrieval quality, while imperfect, is not the binding constraint, and that the bottleneck lies in how the LLM handles what it receives. Stage 3 papers cluster around two distinct sub-problems: the quality and compactness of the context fed to the generator, and the LLM's inherent limitations in processing long inputs.

**Context quality and compression.** Three papers target the gap between retrieved content and what the generator can effectively use. Joren et al. (2025) establish a formal baseline by introducing the concept of sufficient context — whether retrieved content contains enough information to answer the query, independent of LLM capability. Using a Gemini 1.5 Pro autorater with 93% classification accuracy, they find that even when context is sufficient, frontier models hallucinate at 14–17%, and with insufficient context, models hallucinate far more than they abstain. Their selective generation approach — combining a sufficiency signal with model self-confidence — improves accuracy by 2–10% at most coverage levels with no training cost. This work is notable for what it establishes as a baseline: the generation failure rate independent of retrieval quality.

Li and Ramakrishnan (2025) address context quality through a learned reconstruction module. Oreo, a T5-small model trained via SFT → contrastive learning → PPO alignment, sits between retriever and generator and transforms noisy, redundant retrieved chunks into a concise, query-specific context. The three-stage training is motivated by a structural limitation of prior compression approaches: independently trained retriever and generator components create distributional mismatches that gradient backpropagation cannot resolve when the generator is a black box. RL alignment directly optimizes for the generator's output quality. The result is up to 37.5% EM improvement on NaturalQuestions and 84–94% context compression, with latency reduced by 23–43%.

Lin et al. (2025) take an architectural approach to the same problem, motivated by the observation that retrieved passages produce block-diagonal attention patterns (near-zero cross-chunk attention) due to semantic diversity from deduplication. REFRAG compresses retrieved chunks into pre-computable encoder embeddings before decoding, reducing input length by a factor of k. At compression rate k=32, this yields 30.85× TTFT acceleration over standard LLaMA and 3.75× over the previous SOTA, with matching perplexity. Because chunk embeddings are query-independent, the encoding cost is paid once at index time rather than once per inference.

**LLM context utilization limits.** The remaining four Stage 3 papers share a different framing: rather than asking how to improve the context fed to the LLM, they ask whether the LLM can use long context reliably at all — and find that it cannot.

Du et al. (2025) demonstrate this with controlled synthetic experiments across five models and four tasks, verifying retrieval quality by exact match before testing. Even when all distractor tokens are masked from attention — eliminating noise, irrelevance, and the "lost-in-the-middle" effect — accuracy drops consistently as context length grows (at minimum 7.9% for both open-source models at 30k masked tokens). The drop is attributed to positional distribution bias: LLMs trained on short-context data develop positional encodings that degrade when evidence and question are separated by positional distance, regardless of whether intervening tokens are informative. Their retrieve-then-solve mitigation — prompting the model to recite retrieved evidence before solving using only that recited text — recovers up to 31.2% accuracy in some settings.

Roberts et al. (2025) extend this finding to near-million-scale contexts with a synthetic benchmark of progressively difficult tasks (single needle to multi-step threading) across 17 LLMs. Their effective context length metric reveals that most models perform far below their advertised context limits in practice. For multi-step threading at five steps, all models except GPT-4o and LLaMA 3.1 405B achieve zero effective context length. A surprising positive result is that models are "thread-safe" in the synthetic setting, but this advantage disappears in natural language contexts.

Bansal et al. (2025) provide a theoretical account: score dilution in static self-attention causes softmax attention mass on any target token to vanish as context length grows, with the required logit gap scaling as Ω(log T). Thinking tokens cannot fix this — each contributes at most its own attention mass. Their remedy, qTTT, performs a small number of gradient updates on only the query projection matrices W_Q at inference time, while keeping the KV cache frozen. This provably increases the target–distractor logit margin without re-prefilling the full context. On LongBench-v2, qTTT achieves 39.6% accuracy versus 33.5% for FLOP-matched thinking tokens and 27.0% for standard in-context.

Completing this stage, Parametric RAG (Su et al., 2025) takes the most radical Stage 3 intervention: eliminating the retrieved context from the input entirely. Instead, retrieved documents' LoRA adapters (trained at Stage 1) are merged into the LLM's FFN weights before generation, injecting knowledge parametrically rather than contextually. This achieves 29–36% lower inference latency versus standard RAG while outperforming in-context baselines on most benchmarks, and combining both paradigms achieves the highest overall performance.

### 3.4 Cross-Cutting Observations

The pipeline-stage view surfaces two structural observations about this corpus. First, the research weight is heavily concentrated at Stage 3 — seven of eleven papers — while Stage 1 and Stage 2 receive comparatively thin coverage. This reflects an implicit assumption that retrieval is "good enough" and that the binding constraint is in generation-time context utilization. The Stage 2 null results from Li et al. (2025) and the cautionary findings from Vladika and Matthes (2025) partly support this assumption, but the Stage 1 result from Golden-Retriever (35% gain from query rewriting alone) suggests the assumption does not hold in domain-specific settings.

Second, Stage 3 contains an internal tension: the context compression sub-cluster (Oreo, REFRAG) and the LLM utilization limits sub-cluster (Context Length Alone Hurts, Needle Threading, qTTT) are addressing related but distinct problems. Compression assumes the bottleneck is context noise and length — reduce those and generation improves. The utilization limits papers suggest the bottleneck is deeper: positional encoding bias and score dilution that persist even with minimal, clean context. The right Stage 3 intervention thus depends on which failure mode dominates in a given deployment, a question no reviewed paper addresses with comparative experiments across both sub-problems.
